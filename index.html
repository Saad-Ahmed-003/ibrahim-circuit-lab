Ohm's Law — The Formula That Rules All Circuits

In Session 1 you learned the intuition: more voltage → more current, more resistance → less current. Georg Ohm discovered that this relationship is not just vague — it's precise and mathematical. The exact relationship between voltage, current, and resistance is captured in one of the most famous equations in all of science and engineering.

The Ohm's Law triangle. To find any one value: cover it with your finger — the remaining two show you the formula. V (volts) on top, I (amps) and R (ohms) on the bottom.
FIND VOLTAGE
V = I × R
Volts = Amps × Ohms
FIND CURRENT
I = V ÷ R
Amps = Volts ÷ Ohms
FIND RESISTANCE
R = V ÷ I
Ohms = Volts ÷ Amps
1.1 — The LED Circuit Calculation You've Already Been Using

In Session 1 you used a 330Ω resistor with a 9V battery and an LED. Now let's work out exactly what's happening, step by step. LEDs have what's called a forward voltage — this is the voltage the LED itself "uses up" just to light up. Red LEDs use about 2V, green about 2.1V, blue and white about 3.2V. The rest of the battery's voltage is "dropped" across the resistor.

🧮 Example Walkthrough — Red LED + 9V Battery + 330Ω
Step 1: Subtract the LED's forward voltage from the battery voltage.
Voltage across resistor = 9V − 2V = 7V

Step 2: Use Ohm's Law (I = V ÷ R) to find the current.
I = 7V ÷ 330Ω = 0.0212 A = 21.2 mA

Result: 21.2 mA — right in the LED's happy range of 20 mA. ✓ The 330Ω is exactly the right choice.

This is why choosing the right resistor isn't guesswork — it's a quick calculation. Want a dimmer LED? Use a bigger resistor. Want it brighter (carefully — don't go above 25 mA for standard LEDs)? Use a smaller resistor.

1.2 — How to Work Out the Right Resistor Value

If you're starting from scratch — you know your battery voltage, you know your LED's forward voltage, and you want exactly 20 mA — you can rearrange Ohm's Law to find the ideal resistor:

📐 The Resistor Formula
R = (Vbattery − VLED) ÷ Idesired

For a 9V battery, green LED (2.1V forward voltage), 20mA desired current:
R = (9 − 2.1) ÷ 0.020 = 6.9 ÷ 0.020 = 345Ω

Since 345Ω isn't a standard resistor value, you round up to the nearest standard value: 390Ω or use your 330Ω (which gives 20.9mA — perfectly fine).

Series Circuits — One Single Path for Current

So far every circuit you've built has had one component at a time. But real circuits have multiple components. The way those components are connected to each other makes a huge difference — and there are exactly two fundamental ways to connect things: series and parallel.

In a series circuit, components are connected one after the other in a single chain — like carriages on a train. There is only one path for current to flow, so the exact same current flows through every single component. If any part of the chain is broken, the whole circuit stops.

Series circuit with one 330Ω resistor and two LEDs. Current has only one path. Same current through all. The 9V is "shared out" — 2V across each LED, and the rest across the resistor.
2.1 — The Rules of Series Circuits
📏 Series: What Stays the Same
Current (I) is identical everywhere in the loop
Resistances add up: Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3...
Voltage divides across each component
Remove one component → whole circuit stops
More LEDs in series → each gets less voltage
💡 Two LEDs in Series — The Maths
Battery: 9V
LED forward voltage × 2: 2V + 2V = 4V
Voltage left for resistor: 9 − 4 = 5V
With 330Ω: I = 5V ÷ 330Ω = 15.2 mA
✓ Still safe for LEDs (max 25mA)
⚠️ The Series LED Limit
You can keep adding LEDs in series, but each one "uses up" some of the battery voltage. With a 9V battery and red LEDs (2V each), you can fit at most 4 LEDs (4 × 2V = 8V — leaves 1V for the resistor). Try 5 and there's almost no voltage left for the resistor, meaning almost no current flows and the LEDs barely glow. This is why strings of LED fairy lights sometimes go dim at the end.

Parallel Circuits — Multiple Paths for Current

In a parallel circuit, components are connected side by side — like lanes on a motorway. Current splits and takes multiple paths simultaneously. The big difference: every component in a parallel circuit gets the full voltage of the power supply. This is how your house wiring works — every plug socket gets 230V regardless of what's plugged in.

Parallel circuit: two independent LED + resistor branches. Each branch gets the full 9V. Both LEDs glow at the same brightness as a single LED would. The battery just has to supply more total current.
3.1 — The Rules of Parallel Circuits
📐 Parallel: What Stays the Same
Voltage is identical across every branch
Current divides — more branches = more total current from battery
Total resistance decreases (more paths = less total resistance)
Remove one branch → other branches keep working
Adding more branches drains the battery faster
🏠 Why Your House is Wired in Parallel
Every plug socket in your home is wired in parallel. That's why plugging in a second lamp doesn't make both lamps dimmer. Each appliance gets the full 230V. Turning off one appliance doesn't affect the others. The only limit is how much total current your mains circuit can supply before a fuse blows.

Capacitors — The Component That Stores Electricity

Up until now, every component you've used either conducts electricity (wires, resistors) or converts it into something else (LEDs → light). Now you're going to meet something completely different: a component that stores electrical energy and releases it later. This is a capacitor.

A capacitor is two metal plates separated by an insulating material called a dielectric. When you connect a capacitor to a voltage source, electric charges build up on the plates — one side collects positive charges, the other collects negative charges. The capacitor is essentially storing a tiny bit of voltage. When you disconnect the power source, the stored charge stays there — and the capacitor can then release it back out into the circuit.

Inside a capacitor: two conducting plates separated by an insulating dielectric. Charges build up on each plate. The electric field between the plates stores the energy — like a very fast, low-capacity rechargeable battery.
4.1 — Capacitance: The Unit and the Scale

Capacitance is measured in Farads (F), named after Michael Faraday. One Farad is a massive amount of capacitance — in practice almost no capacitor you'll ever use is rated in whole Farads. Instead, you'll almost always see:

📏 Capacitor Units You'll Actually Use
µF (microfarad) = one millionth of a Farad (0.000001 F). Most common for timing circuits. Your kit has 100µF and 1000µF capacitors.

nF (nanofarad) = one billionth of a Farad. Common in audio and radio circuits.

pF (picofarad) = one trillionth of a Farad. Used in high-frequency/radio circuits. Almost invisibly small.

Scale: 1µF = 1,000nF = 1,000,000pF — so a 100µF capacitor stores a hundred million times more charge than a 1pF one.
4.2 — Two Types of Capacitors in Your Kit
ELECTROLYTIC
Cylindrical, usually blue or black · Your 100µF and 1000µF caps

These use a chemical process (electrolysis) to achieve a very large capacitance in a small package. The trade-off: they are polarised — they have a positive and a negative leg, and they must be connected the right way around. The negative leg is marked with a white stripe and a "−" symbol on the body. The positive leg is usually the longer one (like an LED).

Used for: power supply filtering, timing circuits, audio coupling, charge reservoirs.

CERAMIC DISC
Small yellow/brown disc · Your 100nF (0.1µF) cap

These use a ceramic material as the dielectric. They are much smaller in capacitance but have two big advantages: they are not polarised (either leg can go either way), and they respond extremely fast to voltage changes, making them ideal for filtering out high-frequency electrical noise.

Used for: decoupling/bypass capacitors near chips (you'll see one on nearly every Arduino module), RF circuits, signal filtering.

⚠️
Critical: Electrolytic Capacitors Must Go In the Right Way
Unlike resistors (which have no polarity) and even LEDs (which just don't light up backwards), an electrolytic capacitor connected backwards in a circuit can be permanently damaged — and in extreme cases, can rupture and leak or even pop. Always check the stripe: the white band with "−" marks the negative leg. That leg goes to ground. The longer, unmarked leg is positive and goes to the supply side. Check this every single time before powering up.
4.3 — What Capacitors Actually Do in a Circuit

The most important behaviour to understand: a capacitor charges up gradually and discharges gradually. When you first connect a capacitor to a voltage source, current flows in to charge it up — but as the capacitor fills up, the current slows down and eventually stops. When you disconnect the power source and connect the capacitor to a load (like an LED), it releases its charge — but again, gradually, not all at once.

This gradual charge and discharge behaviour is what makes capacitors useful. Here are the main things they do in real circuits:

🔋 The Three Big Jobs of Capacitors
1. Timing: Combine a capacitor with a resistor and you get a predictable delay. The bigger the capacitor or resistor, the longer the delay. This is how timer circuits, oscillators, and clock generators work.

2. Smoothing: If you plug a 9V adapter into the wall, the output isn't perfectly steady — it ripples slightly. A large capacitor across the power supply "fills in" the dips, keeping the voltage smooth. That's why large capacitors appear in almost every power supply.

3. Blocking DC, passing AC: Capacitors don't pass steady direct current (once charged, they block it) but they do pass changing signals (AC). This makes them essential in audio amplifiers — they pass sound signals through while blocking unwanted DC offsets.

RC Circuits — The Maths of Charging and Discharging

When you put a resistor and capacitor together, you get one of the most fundamental building blocks in all of electronics: the RC circuit. The resistor controls how fast the capacitor charges and discharges. This is where the timing behaviour comes from.

5.1 — The Time Constant (τ)

The speed of charging and discharging is described by a single number called the time constant, written with the Greek letter tau: τ (tau). It's calculated like this:

⏱ The Time Constant Formula
τ = R × C
Where R is in Ohms (Ω) and C is in Farads (F). The result τ is in seconds.

After 1τ: capacitor is 63% charged (or 63% discharged).
After 2τ: 86% charged.
After 3τ: 95% charged.
After 5τ: 99.3% charged — considered "fully charged" in practice.
The RC charging curve. Voltage rises quickly at first, then slows as it approaches the supply voltage. At exactly 1τ (one time constant), the capacitor is 63% charged. It never quite reaches 100% mathematically, but at 5τ it's close enough that we treat it as fully charged.

🧪 Mini-Experiment 3
Watch a Capacitor Charge and Discharge Through an LED

This is the most satisfying experiment of the session. You're going to watch a capacitor charge up, then disconnect the power and watch the stored energy slowly fade out through an LED — a glowing, visible demonstration of the time constant in action.

✏️ Calculate First
Using R = 10kΩ and C = 1000µF: τ = R × C = 10,000 × 0.001 = seconds.
So after seconds the capacitor should be ~63% charged.
After 5τ = , it is essentially fully charged.
  1. Build the charge circuit: Place the 10kΩ resistor between the + rail and column 3 row a. Place the 1000µF electrolytic capacitor — positive leg (longer, no stripe) into column 3 row f, negative leg (stripe side) into the − rail. Check polarity carefully! Put one jumper from row a col 3 across the gap to row f col 3 to connect resistor output to the capacitor positive leg.
  2. Connect the battery. Do not connect the LED yet. Wait the full 5τ time you calculated — use a clock or count. The capacitor is now charged.
  3. Disconnect the battery (pull the snap connector off).
  4. Now place a 330Ω resistor and an LED in series, connected across the capacitor legs (positive cap leg → resistor → LED long leg → LED short leg → negative cap leg). The LED should glow!
  5. Watch carefully. Does the LED fade gradually? Time how long it takes to go completely dark.
  6. Recharge the capacitor with the battery (steps 1–3 again). This time, replace the 330Ω with the 10kΩ resistor in the discharge path. Does the LED take longer or shorter to fade?
🔍 What You Should See

The LED should glow brightly at first then slowly fade over several seconds to minutes depending on the resistor. With 330Ω discharge resistor the fade should take roughly 5–10 seconds. With 10kΩ it should take much longer (up to a minute or more). This is the time constant in action — you're literally watching τ = RC happen in front of you. The capacitor was storing real electrical energy and is now releasing it.

🧪 Mini-Experiment 4
The LED Pulse — Charge Fast, Discharge Slow

Now you're going to build a circuit that demonstrates how capacitors can create time-based behaviour automatically — the start of understanding how timers and oscillators work.

  1. Build the two-switch circuit: You'll need two separate paths from your capacitor — one charging path (through 10kΩ from + rail), and one discharge path (330Ω + LED to − rail). Put the charge path and discharge path in parallel but connected to the same capacitor. Both should be connected simultaneously.
  2. Connect the battery. What happens? The capacitor charges through the 10kΩ, and the LED shows the discharge state — watch carefully to see if there's any glow behaviour.
  3. Now temporarily touch a jumper wire from the capacitor's positive leg directly to the + rail (bypassing the 10kΩ). This is a "fast charge". Remove it after 1 second. Watch the LED.
  4. Repeatedly: fast charge (touch wire), wait (watch LED fade), fast charge again. You've created a manual pulse generator.
  5. Swap the 1000µF cap for the 100µF cap. Does the fade happen faster or slower? By how much? Use your τ = RC calculator to predict first.

💡 Hint: Why Your Time Might Be Off
Real capacitors have ±20% tolerance (much less precise than resistors). A 100µF cap might actually be anywhere from 80µF to 120µF. The LED also has resistance — roughly 50–100Ω depending on current. Both of these affect your actual τ. If you want better accuracy, measure your capacitor with the multimeter's capacitance mode if it has one.
Part C — The Extension Challenge ⭐⭐

Can you set up the circuit so that it automatically recharges through a resistor while discharging through the LED — making the LED slowly breathe (fade in, fade out, fade in, fade out) without you doing anything? You'll need to think about charge and discharge paths and how the resistors affect each. Sketch your circuit on paper before you build it. There is no single answer — experiment and observe.

🧠 What You're Actually Building
What you just designed in Part C is a primitive version of something called a relaxation oscillator — a circuit that repeatedly charges and discharges a capacitor to create a repeating signal. Real oscillators (used to create clock signals in microcontrollers and computers) use the exact same principle, just much faster (millions of times per second) and with transistors to switch the charge/discharge paths. You now understand the core idea behind all of them.
