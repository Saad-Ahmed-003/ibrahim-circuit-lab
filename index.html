1
What Is Electricity? — The Real Story

Electricity isn't magic. It isn't invisible fire or some mysterious force. It's actually something incredibly simple once you zoom in far enough — it's just tiny particles moving through a material. But to understand that, we need to start even smaller.

1.1 — Everything Is Made of Atoms

Every single thing around you — the table, the wire, the air, your hand — is made of incredibly tiny things called atoms. They're so small that if you lined up a hundred million atoms side by side, they'd still only be about 1 centimeter wide. You cannot see them even with the best microscopes in schools.

An atom has three parts:

An atom has a nucleus in the middle (with protons and neutrons) and electrons orbiting around it. The outer electrons are the ones that matter for electricity.

The nucleus in the middle is made of protons (which carry a positive charge) and neutrons (no charge). Orbiting around the nucleus are electrons, which carry a negative charge. Normally, the positive protons and negative electrons balance each other out — the atom is neutral.

1.2 — Free Electrons and Why Metals Are Special

Here's where it gets interesting. In most materials, the electrons are tightly held to their atoms — they can't go anywhere. These materials are called insulators. Plastic, rubber, wood, glass — their electrons don't move easily.

But in metals like copper, silver, and gold, the outer electrons are only loosely attached to their atoms. They're so loosely attached that they basically float freely between atoms — bouncing around randomly all the time, not belonging to any particular atom. We call these free electrons.

This is exactly why metals conduct electricity and plastic doesn't. It's not magic — it's just about whether the electrons can move or not.

🧠 A Good Way to Picture It
Imagine a hallway packed with people standing shoulder to shoulder. In a conductor (metal), everyone is standing on roller skates — a tiny push and they all move. In an insulator (plastic), everyone is glued to the floor — you can push as hard as you want and nothing moves.
1.3 — What Electricity Actually Is

When you connect a battery to a wire, the battery creates a push — it forces electrons to move in one direction through the wire. This stream of electrons moving through the wire is what we call electric current. That's literally it. Electricity = electrons moving.

Here's something cool: the electrons don't actually travel all the way from one end of the wire to the other quickly. What actually happens is more like a chain reaction — imagine that hallway packed full of people on roller skates again. If you push the person at one end, they bump into the next person, who bumps into the next, all the way to the other end almost instantly. Each person only moved a tiny bit, but the effect traveled the whole length at nearly the speed of light.

💡 Why Does a Wire Get Warm?
As electrons move through a wire, they bump into the atoms. Every collision causes a tiny bit of vibration in the atoms — and vibration is heat. This is why wires get warm when current flows through them. The more current (more electrons moving), the more collisions, the more heat. This is also exactly why resistors can get quite hot — they intentionally create lots of those collisions to limit the flow.
2
Conductors, Insulators — and Everything In Between

Now that you know electricity is electrons moving, you can understand exactly why some things conduct electricity and some don't.

⚡ Good Conductors

  • Copper (most wires are copper)
  • Aluminum (some wires, foil)
  • Gold (used on circuit boards)
  • Silver (best conductor)
  • Iron / Steel
  • Graphite (pencil lead!)
  • Saltwater
  • The human body (yes, really!)

🚫 Good Insulators

  • Plastic (wire insulation)
  • Rubber (gloves, boots)
  • Glass
  • Wood (when dry)
  • Air (usually)
  • Ceramic
  • Paper
  • Pure water (no minerals)

You'll notice graphite is on the conductor list — that's the black stuff inside a pencil. It's not actually lead (despite being called "pencil lead"). It's a form of carbon called graphite, and it conducts electricity pretty well. You'll use this in Mini-Experiment 2 later.

And yes — the human body conducts electricity. This is why you can get an electric shock and also why electricians wear rubber-soled boots. Your body has salt water in it, and salt water conducts. With the low voltages we use today (9V), you won't feel anything — but this becomes very important to know when working with mains electricity (the sockets in the wall) one day.

⚠️ Semiconductors — A Sneak Peek
There's actually a third category called semiconductors. These materials are between conductors and insulators — they don't conduct well normally, but you can control how much they conduct by applying voltage or adding other atoms to them. Silicon is the most famous semiconductor. Your phone, your Arduino, every computer chip ever made — they're all built from semiconductors. You'll learn about transistors (which are semiconductor devices) in a few sessions.

Build a Conductivity Tester

You're going to build a simple circuit that acts like a "does this thing conduct electricity?" detector. When you touch the probes to something that conducts, the LED lights up. When you touch something that doesn't conduct, it stays dark.

How it works: You're creating a circuit that's missing a piece — the gap between the two probe wires. If the object you're testing conducts electricity, it completes the circuit and current flows through the LED. Simple!

  2. Take another jumper wire and connect it to the − power rail. That's your second probe.
  3. Connect the battery. The LED should be off (the circuit is open — there's a gap).
  4. Now touch the two free wire ends (probes) together. The LED should light up — the circuit is complete.

3
Voltage, Current, and Resistance — The Full Story

These three things are the language of all electronics. Every circuit, every component, every calculation you'll ever do uses these three. You'll hear them hundreds of times from now on, so let's make sure you understand each one deeply — not just the definition, but the why behind it.

VOLTAGE
Symbol: V  ·  Unit: Volts (V)
Voltage is the electrical pressure that pushes electrons through a wire. Without voltage, electrons have no reason to move — they just sit there. A higher voltage means a stronger push. A 9V battery pushes harder than a 1.5V AA battery.
Water analogy: voltage is the height difference between a water tank and a pipe below. The higher the tank, the more pressure the water has at the bottom.
CURRENT
Symbol: I  ·  Unit: Amps (A), or milliamps (mA)
Current is how many electrons are actually flowing through a wire every second. 1 Amp = roughly 6 billion billion electrons per second. For LEDs and small circuits, we usually work in milliamps — 1 mA = one thousandth of an amp. Most LEDs are happy with 20 mA.
Water analogy: current is how much water is actually flowing through the pipe every second — like liters per minute.
RESISTANCE
Symbol: R  ·  Unit: Ohms (Ω)
Resistance is how hard it is for electrons to flow through something. Every material has some resistance. Resistors are components designed to have a specific, predictable resistance — so you can control how much current flows in a circuit.
Water analogy: resistance is a narrow section in the pipe, or a sponge blocking the way. It slows down the flow.
3.1 — How They're Connected

These three things aren't independent — they're linked together. If you know two of them, you can always figure out the third. Georg Ohm discovered the exact relationship in 1827, and it became one of the most important rules in all of electronics. You'll learn the formula properly in Session 3, but here's the key idea:

🔗 The Connection
More voltage → more current flows (stronger push = more flow)
More resistance → less current flows (harder to push through = less flow)
Both together: if you double the voltage AND double the resistance at the same time, the current stays the same!

This is why you can change the brightness of an LED by changing either the battery voltage OR the resistor value. Both affect the current through the LED.
3.2 — A Better Analogy: The Water System
High tank = battery voltage (pressure). Narrow section in pipe = resistor (slows flow). The wheel being spun by water = LED being lit by current. Water returns to the low tank = ground wire back to negative battery terminal.

The water tank analogy isn't just a metaphor — it maps perfectly to real circuit behavior. A battery is essentially a pump that constantly moves water from the low tank to the high tank, maintaining the pressure difference. When you connect a circuit, water (current) flows from high pressure to low pressure, doing work (spinning the wheel, lighting the LED) along the way.

4
Inside an LED — Why It Actually Glows

LED stands for Light Emitting Diode. Let's break that apart because every word matters.

💡 LIGHT EMITTING

When current flows through the LED's special material, electrons move from a higher-energy area to a lower-energy area inside the material. When they "fall" to the lower energy level, they release that extra energy — and it comes out as light. Different materials produce different colors because different materials release different amounts of energy, and different amounts of energy = different colors of light.

⚡ DIODE

A diode is a component that only allows current to flow in one direction. Think of it like a one-way door — it lets electrons through going one way, but blocks them completely going the other way. This is why the LED has a positive leg (long) and a negative leg (short). If you put it in backwards, current can't flow and it won't light up. (It also won't be harmed by this — more on that in a minute.)

🔴 WHY DIFFERENT COLORS?

Red LEDs use a material that releases a small amount of energy when electrons fall — small energy = low frequency = red light. Blue LEDs require electrons to fall a larger energy gap, releasing more energy = higher frequency = blue light. This is why blue LEDs were harder to invent and why their inventors won the Nobel Prize in 2014. Different colors have different forward voltages too.

☠️ WHY LEDs BURN OUT

When too much current flows through an LED, too many electrons are crashing through at once. All those collisions generate heat inside the tiny chip. The LED material can only handle so much heat before it physically melts or breaks. Once it's burnt, it's gone — you can't fix it. This is exactly why the resistor is non-negotiable.

The long leg is always + (anode). The short leg, and the flat side of the plastic body, is always − (cathode). Current can only flow from + to −. Inside the dome is a tiny chip of semiconductor material that emits light when current flows through it.
🧪 Mini-Experiment 2
What Happens When You Flip the LED Backwards?

You know that an LED is a diode — it only lets current flow one way. Let's actually see what happens when you put it in the wrong way.

  1. Build your normal LED + 330Ω resistor circuit on the breadboard. Power it on. LED should be on.
  2. Disconnect the battery. Now pull the LED out and put it back in the other way around — swap the long leg and short leg positions.
  3. Reconnect the battery. What happens?
  4. Disconnect the battery again and put the LED back the correct way. It should light up normally again.
🔍 What You Should See

When the LED is backwards, it doesn't light up at all — current simply can't flow through it in that direction. And importantly, the LED is not damaged by this. You can flip it back and it works fine. This is actually a useful trick: if an LED doesn't light up, try flipping it around — it might just be in backwards. This will happen to everyone at some point, including experienced engineers.

5
Why the Resistor Is Non-Negotiable

You've already heard that LEDs need resistors. Now you know enough to understand exactly why — and what actually happens if you skip the resistor.

A standard LED is designed to operate at around 20 milliamps (0.020 A) of current. That's its "happy zone" — it lights up brightly and will last for thousands of hours. Now think about what happens with a 9V battery and no resistor:

The battery pushes with 9 volts. The LED's internal resistance is extremely small — just a few ohms. So the current that would flow is enormous — potentially hundreds of milliamps. That's 10 to 20 times what the LED is designed for. In a fraction of a second, the tiny chip inside gets so hot that it burns out. Sometimes you see a tiny flash as it dies. Sometimes it just quietly stops working. Either way, it's gone.

Left: without a resistor, the 9V battery tries to push way too much current through the LED — it burns out almost immediately. Right: with a 330Ω resistor, the current is limited to ~21mA — right in the LED's happy range.
📌 The Resistor's Job — In One Sentence
The resistor makes it hard enough for current to flow that only the right amount gets through — protecting the LED while still giving it enough current to glow brightly.

The resistor gets warm doing this job — it absorbs the extra electrical energy and converts it to heat. If you ever feel a resistor in a circuit gently with your finger (while powered by a 9V battery), you'll notice it's slightly warm. That warmth is the resistor doing its job.

6
The Breadboard — Every Hole Explained

Before breadboards existed, engineers had to solder every single connection — solder is a special metal you melt to join wires permanently. For testing and learning, this was incredibly slow and frustrating. The breadboard was invented so you can push component legs into holes, try things out, and pull them out and rearrange as many times as you want.

6.1 — What's Inside the Breadboard?

Underneath the plastic surface of a breadboard are long metal spring clips. Each hole on the surface connects to one of these clips. The clips are what actually make the electrical connections — when you push a wire into a hole, the spring clips grab it and hold it firmly, making a good electrical contact.

Orange column: a3–e3 are all connected vertically. Blue columns: f5,g5 are connected, and f6,g6 are connected — but NOT to the top bank (center gap separates them). The + and − power rails run the entire horizontal length of the breadboard.
6.2 — The Three Zones of a Breadboard

Zone 1 — The power rails (+ and −): The long strips at the very top and bottom edges. Every single hole in the + rail is connected to every other hole in the + rail. Same for the − rail. These are where you connect your battery so that power is available across the whole breadboard.

Zone 2 — The top bank (rows a–e): The upper half of the main area. Holes in the same column number are connected to each other — but only within their bank. So a3, b3, c3, d3, e3 are all connected to each other. But a3 is NOT connected to a4 (different column) and NOT connected to f3 (other bank).

Zone 3 — The bottom bank (rows f–j): Mirror image of the top bank. Same rules apply, but totally separate from the top bank.

🔑 The Most Important Rule
The center gap physically separates the two banks. No connection crosses it. This is intentional — when you later plug a chip (like an Arduino's microcontroller) across the gap, each leg of the chip sits in its own bank and can be wired independently.
🧪 Mini-Experiment 3
Map the Breadboard with Your Multimeter

The best way to truly understand the breadboard is to probe it yourself. You'll use your multimeter in continuity mode — this mode beeps when the two probes are connected. (If your multimeter doesn't have a continuity mode with a beep, use resistance mode — connected holes will show near 0Ω, disconnected holes will show "1" or "OL" meaning infinite resistance.)

Setting up continuity mode: Turn the dial to the symbol that looks like a speaker or sound wave (sometimes combined with the diode symbol ⟶|). Touch the probes together — it should beep.

  1. Put one probe in hole a3. Put the other probe in hole b3 (same column, one row down). Does it beep? It should — they're connected.
  2. Keep one probe in a3. Move the other to a4 (same row, one column over). Does it beep? It shouldn't — different column.
  3. Now test e3 vs f3. This is the gap test — do they connect? They should NOT beep.
  4. Test two holes in the + power rail: any two holes along the red + strip. Do they beep? They should — the entire + rail is one connection.
  5. Test the + rail against the − rail. Do they beep? They absolutely should NOT — that would mean your battery would be short-circuited.
  6. Try a few more combinations of your own. Write down what you find.
🔍 What This Tells You

After this experiment, the breadboard connection rules won't be something you just memorize — you'll have physically confirmed them yourself. Every time you build a circuit now, you'll instinctively know which holes connect and which don't. That's the real goal here.

7
Building Your First Real Circuit

You've done the experiments, you understand electricity, you understand the breadboard. Now let's put it all together and build a proper circuit — the right way.

⚠️ The Golden Rule of Circuit Building
Always connect the battery LAST — after everything else is in place. And always disconnect the battery FIRST before making any changes. This protects your components from accidental short-circuits while you're moving things around.
7.1 — Understanding the Circuit Path

Before placing anything, let's understand the path the current will take. Electricity always needs a complete loop — from the battery's positive (+) terminal, through the circuit, and back to the negative (−) terminal. If there's any break in the loop, no current flows.

Our circuit path: Battery + → wire to + rail → resistor → LED (+ leg) → LED (− leg) → wire to − rail → Battery −

The complete circuit. Red wire = positive path. Orange bridge wire = from resistor to LED. Blue wire = back to ground. The battery connects to the power rails on the sides.
  1. Place the resistor: Push one leg into row a, column 3 and the other leg into row a, column 5. The resistor body sits flat across the two columns.
  2. Red jumper wire: Connect a hole in the + power rail to column 3, row a (where the resistor leg is). This connects battery power to the resistor.
  3. Yellow/orange bridge wire: Connect column 5 row a (the other resistor leg) to column 5 row f on the bottom bank. This carries current across the gap to the LED side.
  4. Place the LED: Long leg (+) into column 5 row f. Short leg (−) into column 7 row f. The LED should straddle across two columns.
  5. Black jumper wire: Connect column 7 row f (short LED leg) down to the − power rail.
  6. Battery last: Snap the 9V battery connector on. Red wire to a hole in the + rail, black wire to a hole in the − rail. The LED should light up.
💡 LED Not Lighting Up? Troubleshoot Like This:
1. Check the LED direction — try flipping it around.
2. Check that every connection is actually pushed in firmly — a half-pushed wire doesn't make contact.
3. Check that your bridge wire actually goes from the resistor's second leg column to the LED's long-leg column.
4. Make sure the + rail and − rail aren't connected to each other (that would short-circuit the battery).
⚡ Session Challenge
Three Experiments — One After Another

Your LED circuit is working. Now you're going to run three back-to-back experiments to see the physics in action. Do them in order — each one builds on the last.

Experiment A — The Resistor Dimmer
  1. Your circuit currently has a 330Ω resistor. Note the LED brightness. Disconnect the battery.
  2. Swap the 330Ω for a 1kΩ resistor (1000 Ohms). Reconnect the battery. Is the LED brighter or dimmer? Why?
  3. Disconnect again. Swap for a 10kΩ resistor (10,000 Ohms). Reconnect. What happened to the LED now?
  4. Put the 330Ω back. Confirm the LED is bright again.
📊 The Pattern You're Seeing
330Ω → bright. 1kΩ → dimmer. 10kΩ → very dim or barely visible. Higher resistance = less current = less light. This is Ohm's Law in action — you'll calculate the exact numbers in Session 3.
Experiment B — The Pencil Variable Resistor
✏️
Graphite (the stuff in a pencil) conducts electricity — but not perfectly. It has resistance. And here's the clever part: the longer the path through the graphite, the more resistance it has. A short path across the pencil mark = low resistance. A long path = high resistance. You can use a pencil to make a variable resistor — one where the resistance changes depending on where you put the probes.
  1. On a piece of paper, draw a thick dark rectangle about 8cm long and 2cm wide using a soft pencil (2B or darker if you have one — the darker the better, more graphite). Fill it in solidly — go over it multiple times to get a thick layer.
  2. Put your 330Ω resistor back in the circuit. Remove the LED for now (just pull it out). Connect the two probe wires from your circuit (the gap-tester from earlier) to two points on the graphite rectangle. If you put them close together, what happens?
  3. Move the probes further apart on the graphite. What changes about the LED brightness?
  4. Try sliding one probe from one end of the rectangle to the other continuously. Can you make the LED slowly fade?
💡 What You Just Made
You built a primitive version of something called a potentiometer (or "pot" for short) — a component with a variable resistance you can adjust. Inside a volume knob on a speaker, inside a dimmer switch for lights, inside a joystick — there's a potentiometer. The physics is exactly what you just saw: change the length of the resistive path, change the resistance, change the current, change the brightness.
Experiment C — How Warm Does the Resistor Get?
  1. Put the LED and the 330Ω resistor back in the circuit. Connect the battery and let it run for about 60 seconds.
  2. Disconnect the battery, then very gently touch the resistor with your fingertip. Is it warm?
  3. Now try this with the 10kΩ resistor instead. After 60 seconds, touch it. Is it warmer or cooler than the 330Ω was?
🔍 What's Happening + Which Gets Hotter?
Surprisingly, the 330Ω resistor gets warmer than the 10kΩ, even though 10kΩ has more resistance. Here's why: the 330Ω lets more current through, so there are more electron collisions per second = more heat generated per second. The 10kΩ barely lets any current through, so it barely heats up. The heat depends on both the current AND the resistance — you'll calculate this exactly when you learn about power (P = I² × R) in a future session.
